A Journey Through Time

The Timeline of English

450
–
1100
Period I
Old English
Anglo-Saxon tribes — the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes — arrived in Britain and brought a Germanic tongue that would become the foundation of English. The language of warriors and poets, it bears little resemblance to the English spoken today.
hus → house
cild → child
mann → man
wæter → water
"Hwæt! We Gardena in geardagum..." — Beowulf, the oldest English epic poem.
1100
–
1500
Period II
Middle English
The Norman Conquest of 1066 shook the English language to its core. French became the language of the court; English that of the common people. Over centuries they blended — producing a richer, more flexible tongue. Geoffrey Chaucer wrote his masterpiece in this era.
kyng → king
lyf → life
sonne → sun
beautee → beauty
"Whan that Aprill with his shoures soote..." — Canterbury Tales, Chaucer.
1500
–
1700
Period III
Early Modern English
The Renaissance and the printing press (1440) standardised spelling and spread literacy. William Shakespeare coined hundreds of words still used today. The King James Bible (1611) shaped the moral vocabulary of the English-speaking world.
bedroom (Shakespeare)
lonely (Shakespeare)
generous (new word)
negotiate (new word)
"All the world's a stage, and all the men and women merely players." — Shakespeare.
1700
–
Now
Period IV
Modern English
English became the global language of empire, science, trade, and technology. Samuel Johnson's Dictionary (1755) and Noah Webster's American Dictionary (1828) cemented two major variants. Today over 1.5 billion people speak English — the world's most widely used language.
colour → color (US)
lift → elevator (US)
selfie (2013)
blog (1999)
From the moon landing to the internet — Modern English documents humanity's greatest achievements.

The Shapers of a Language

Important Figures

Geoffrey Chaucer
c. 1343 – 1400
Often called the "Father of English Literature," Chaucer elevated Middle English to a language worthy of great poetry through his Canterbury Tales.
He introduced over 2,000 words, including "beautiful," "universe," and "deadline."
William Shakespeare
1564 – 1616
The most influential writer in English. He coined over 1,700 new words and wrote 37 plays that defined English literature for centuries.
Words he invented: "bedroom," "lonely," "eyeball," "luggage," and "uncomfortable."
Samuel Johnson
1709 – 1784
Author of A Dictionary of the English Language (1755) — the first comprehensive English dictionary, completed largely alone in nine years.
He defined "lexicographer" as "a writer of dictionaries, a harmless drudge."
Noah Webster
1758 – 1843
The father of American English. His 1828 dictionary standardised American spelling, deliberately simplifying British forms (colour → color).
He personally interviewed thousands of people to document American usage and pronunciation.
William Caxton
c. 1422 – 1491
Introduced the printing press to England in 1476, standardising English spelling and grammar for the first time in history.
He printed over 100 books in 14 years — more than had existed in England before his arrival.
King James I
1566 – 1625
Commissioned the King James Bible (1611) — arguably the most influential book in shaping Modern English language and culture.
The KJV used 8,000 unique words and remains the most-printed book in English history.
J.R.R. Tolkien
1892 – 1973
Oxford philologist and author who created fictional languages rooted in Old English, helping popularise Old English studies in the 20th century.
Tolkien translated Beowulf and wrote scholarly papers on Old English before creating Middle-earth.
Bede the Venerable
c. 672 – 735
Anglo-Saxon monk who wrote the first history of England. His work preserved crucial knowledge of Old English culture and early language development.
He popularised the BC / AD dating system still used worldwide today.
